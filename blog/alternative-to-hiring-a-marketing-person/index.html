Owner's math

The alternative to hiring a marketing person

KitFire · July 2026 · 5 min read

You know you need marketing. You also know what a salary costs. So the decision sits in the drawer with the other someday-problems, and the website stays the way it was five years ago. Here's the honest version of the choice you're actually facing.

The four options everyone knows about

Option 1: The full-time hire

By the time you add payroll taxes, benefits, software, and the management time nobody counts, a real marketing hire is a serious annual commitment — usually the price of a good truck, every year, forever. And here's the part that stings: one person can't be good at everything you need. Web design, SEO, writing, Google Business Profile, analytics — that's four or five different specialties. A single junior generalist does each of them at 60%.

Option 2: The freelancer patchwork

Cheaper, flexible — and it puts you in a job you didn't apply for: marketing project manager. You find them, brief them, chase them, and glue their work together. When your web person, your SEO person, and your content person have never spoken to each other, you're the one keeping the story straight. Most owners quietly stop after a few months, not because the freelancers were bad, but because the coordination cost their evenings.

Option 3: The agency retainer

Agencies solve the specialty problem — and bill accordingly, because you're paying for account managers, meetings, and office space along with the work. Small accounts get junior staff and template strategies. If your monthly budget has three zeroes instead of four, you are nobody's priority client, and the monthly report is often more polished than the results.

Option 4: Do it yourself at 9pm

The most common choice, and the most expensive one in disguise. Your evenings are the scarcest resource in the business. Marketing done in exhausted spare hours is sporadic, and sporadic marketing compounds at exactly zero percent. Google rewards consistency; so do customers.

The fourth-and-a-half option: done-for-you

What's changed in the last two years is that AI can now do the labor of that four-specialty department — build and update the website, tune the SEO weekly, write the posts and articles, keep the Google profile current — while one experienced human directs it, reviews everything, and signs off before anything goes public.

That's the model KitFire runs. It behaves like a marketing department: it works every week whether you thought about marketing or not. It costs like software: a flat $1,750 a month, less than any part-time hire. And your involvement is the part only you can do — answering questions about your business and approving work.

The questions to ask any option

  1. What ships every week? Not "what's tracked" — what gets built, written, published, or fixed.
  2. Who reviews the work before it goes public? If the answer is "you do, if you catch it," keep looking.
  3. Does it get smarter about my business over time? The right answer involves your corrections being remembered, not re-explained every month.
  4. What happens if I stop? You should own your website, your content, and your Google profile outright. Walk away from anyone who holds them hostage.

Whatever you choose, choose something that runs weekly. The businesses pulling ahead in your county aren't out-spending you — they're out-consistent-ing you.
