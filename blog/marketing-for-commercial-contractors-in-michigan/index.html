Marketing for commercial contractors in Michigan: a practical playbook

KitFire · July 2026 · 6 min read

Your best work is bolted to a wall in a school gym three counties away, and the facility manager who needs exactly that work next month has never heard your name. That's the whole marketing problem for commercial contractors, and most of the advice out there — post more, boost this, buy ads — doesn't fix it.

Here's what actually moves the needle, in the order it matters.

1. Understand how commercial buyers actually find you

Residential customers scroll and impulse-click. Commercial buyers — facility managers, school business officials, general contractors, property managers — behave differently. They search when a problem shows up (a failed inspection, a renovation budget, a bid package), they search specifically ("gym equipment installation Michigan", "bleacher inspection near me"), and they vet before they call. Your job isn't to interrupt them; it's to be findable and credible at the exact moment they go looking.

That means three surfaces have to be right: your website, your Google presence, and — increasingly — what AI assistants say when someone asks them for a recommendation.

2. Make your website prove scope, not just existence

Most contractor sites are a phone number, a stock photo, and a paragraph from 2014. A commercial buyer looks at that and can't answer their three questions: Do you do this kind of work? Have you done it at my scale? Do you work where I am?

The fix is specificity:

  • A page per service. Not "Services" — a page for each thing you bid on, written in the words your buyers use.
  • Real projects with real places. A project gallery naming the school, city, or facility (with permission) does double duty: it convinces the human and it tells Google you work in Whitmore Lake, Saginaw, and Grand Rapids — which is how you show up for "near me" and city-specific searches far from your shop.
  • Proof of coverage. If you work statewide, show it — a project map beats the sentence "serving all of Michigan" every time.
  • A phone number and a human name. Commercial buyers don't fill out mystery forms. They call the person whose name is on the job.

3. Treat Google Business Profile like a bid document

For local searches, your Google Business Profile often shows up before your website does. Most contractors set it up once and never touch it. Keep the categories exact, the service area honest, the photos recent (job-site photos, not logos), and post occasionally — a finished project with a school name is a perfect post. Reviews from commercial clients carry serious weight here; a short email to a happy facility manager after handoff is the cheapest marketing you'll ever do.

4. Get found in AI search — because your buyers are already asking

Facility managers and GCs have started asking ChatGPT, Claude, and Google's AI results things like "who installs and inspects gym bleachers in Michigan?" AI assistants answer from what they can read: crawlable websites with specific services, cities, and project histories, plus consistent business information across the web. Vague sites are invisible to them for the same reason they're unconvincing to humans. We wrote a full guide on this: How to get your business found in AI search results.

5. Publish answers, not announcements

A blog isn't for "company news." It's for capturing searches that happen before the buyer knows who to call: "how often do bleachers need inspection", "gym floor replacement cost factors", "who's responsible for playground equipment maintenance in schools." One well-written answer per month beats daily social posts, because a good answer keeps ranking — and keeps getting cited by AI tools — for years.

6. Skip the stuff that doesn't work

  • Broad paid ads. Bidding on "contractor Michigan" against national lead-gen sites burns money. Commercial work is won on specificity and proof, not impressions.
  • Generic social media. Your buyer is not hiring you off an inspirational quote graphic. A weekly LinkedIn post about real work, in your voice, is plenty.
  • SEO packages that report "activity." If a vendor's monthly report is about keywords "tracked" rather than pages built and rankings moved, you're buying paperwork.

The honest constraint: nobody on your crew has time for this

Every item above is straightforward. None of it is hard the way fieldwork is hard. It just requires steady weekly hours that owner-operators don't have — which is why it never happens, and why the contractor with a mediocre crew and a great website keeps getting the calls.
