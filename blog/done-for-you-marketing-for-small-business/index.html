The model

Done-for-you marketing for small business: what it is, and when it makes sense

KitFire · July 2026 · 5 min read

"Done-for-you" gets stamped on everything from $99 template shops to five-figure agency retainers, so the phrase has stopped meaning anything on its own. Here's a working definition you can hold vendors to — ours included.

The definition that matters

Done-for-you marketing means the work ships without you doing it, managing it, or remembering it. Not "we give you the tools." Not "we coach your team." The website gets built, the SEO gets tuned, the content gets written and published, the Google profile stays current — and your total involvement is answering questions about your business and approving what goes public.

If you're assembling freelancers, sitting in weekly agency calls, or being handed a "content calendar" to execute, someone has quietly handed the job back to you. That's done-with-you, at best.

What a real done-for-you service must include

  • The website itself. Marketing that points at a broken website is a hose attached to nothing. If a provider won't touch your site, everything else they do leaks.
  • Weekly SEO work. Not an "audit" — ongoing tuning: meta tags, long-tail keyword pages, fixes as Google shifts. Search position is a maintenance discipline, like anything else you own.
  • Content that answers customer questions — blog articles and posts that sound like you wrote them, about your actual work, because that's what both Google and AI search feed on.
  • Google Business Profile upkeep. For local businesses this is often the first impression — and the most commonly abandoned one.
  • A human approval gate. Nothing public without your sign-off. "Done for you" must never mean "done to you."
  • Plain-English reporting. What shipped, what moved, what's next — readable in two minutes, no dashboard login required.

Why this exists now (and mostly didn't before)

The honest reason done-for-you at small-business prices used to be impossible: the labor cost more than small businesses could pay. A service that builds sites, writes weekly, and tunes SEO takes a multi-specialty team, and teams bill like teams — hence agencies pricing small accounts out, or serving them with templates.

AI changed the labor math. The production work — building, writing, tuning, monitoring — can now be done by AI systems running on a deep file about your specific business, while an experienced human directs the strategy and reviews every piece before it ships. Department-level output, software-level price. That's not a slogan; it's just the new cost structure, and it's why KitFire's flat $1,750/month plan can include what it includes.

One thing to demand: memory

The gap between mediocre and excellent done-for-you service is whether it learns. When you correct a phrase, kill an idea, or explain how your customers actually talk — does that correction persist, or will you repeat it next quarter to a new account manager? Ask the question directly: "If I tell you we never use the word 'cheap,' where does that live?" A good answer describes a permanent record that every future piece of work runs on. That's how month twelve sounds more like you than month one, instead of the usual opposite.

Red flags, regardless of vendor

  • You can't leave cleanly. Your domain, site, content, and Google profile should be yours outright, always.
  • Activity reports instead of shipped work. "Keywords tracked" and "impressions served" are what vendors show when nothing got built.
  • No approval step. If things get published without your eyes on them, your reputation is being autopiloted.
  • The same strategy for a bakery and an excavator. If the plan doesn't depend on your niche, it isn't a plan.

When done-for-you is the wrong choice

Fair is fair:

  • You genuinely enjoy marketing and have the hours. Some owners do their own well. If it's already working and you don't resent the evenings, keep going.
  • Your sales are purely relationship-driven — a handful of accounts, all won at dinners. Fix your online presence eventually, but it's not your bottleneck.
  • You want an in-house brand team. If you're scaling toward a real marketing department, hire the department. Done-for-you is for the years when that math doesn't work — see the alternative to hiring a marketing person.

The fit test

Done-for-you makes sense when three things are true: your work is good, your online presence doesn't show it, and the person who should fix that (you) is the same person running the crew, the books, and the schedule. If that's you, the model was built for you.
