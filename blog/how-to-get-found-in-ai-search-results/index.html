AI search

How to get your business found in AI search results

KitFire · July 2026 · 6 min read

A growing share of your customers no longer type "plumber near me" into Google and scroll. They ask ChatGPT, Claude, or Google's AI-generated answer box a full question — "who does commercial gym equipment installation in mid-Michigan?" — and get back three names. Either you're one of the three, or the conversation is over before you knew it started.

How AI assistants pick who to recommend

There's no secret ranking dashboard and nobody to pay for placement. AI systems recommend businesses based on what they can read and verify: your website, your Google Business Profile, directories, reviews, and anywhere else your business is described in text. Two things follow from that:

  • If the information about you is thin, vague, or inconsistent, AI tools skip you — not out of judgment, but because they can't confidently say what you do or where.
  • Everything that makes you legible to AI also makes you rank better in ordinary Google search. This isn't a separate discipline; it's the same work done properly.

The playbook

1. Say precisely what you do, where

AI answers are assembled from specific claims. "Quality solutions for all your needs" gives an AI nothing to work with. "We install, repair, and inspect gymnasium equipment for schools across Michigan" is a sentence an assistant can repeat to a customer. Audit your homepage: could a stranger — human or machine — list your services and service area after thirty seconds? If not, rewrite until they could.

2. Put your service area in writing, not just in your head

Assistants answer location questions with location evidence: city names in project descriptions, a service-area page, a map of completed jobs. If everything on your site points at one address, you'll only ever be recommended within a few miles of it — even if you drive statewide every week.

3. Use structured data

Schema.org markup (LocalBusiness or ProfessionalService, Service, FAQPage) is machine-readable labeling for your pages — it states your name, services, area, and prices in a format built for software to consume. Invisible to visitors, load-bearing for machines. Most small-business sites don't have it, which is exactly why it's an edge.

4. Answer real questions on your site

People ask AI assistants questions, so assistants favor sources that answer questions. A page that directly, plainly answers "how often should bleachers be inspected?" or "what does a commercial roof replacement involve?" is the raw material AI answers are made of. This is what a blog is actually for — not company news. One genuinely useful answer per month builds a library that both Google and AI tools draw from for years.

5. Keep your Google Business Profile and reviews alive

AI tools lean on Google's business data and review text heavily for local recommendations. An abandoned profile with three reviews and no photos reads as "possibly defunct." Current photos, accurate categories and hours, and a steady trickle of real reviews read as "established and active" — to machines and humans alike.

6. Be consistent everywhere

Same business name, same phone number, same service description across your site, Google, Facebook, and directories. Contradictory information makes machines hedge, and a hedging machine recommends someone else.

What doesn't work

You can't buy your way in, keyword-stuff your way in, or prompt-trick your way in. There's no "AI SEO hack" — there's only being the business about which the most specific, consistent, verifiable information exists. That's good news if you do real work, because the playbook above is just documenting reality thoroughly.

The catch: it's maintenance, not a project

Every step here is doable. None of it stays done. Pages need updating as your work changes, answers need writing steadily, profiles need tending, and the businesses winning AI recommendations are the ones doing it weekly. That's the "never clocks out" part — and it's precisely the job KitFire runs for owner-operators who have a business to run instead. Your site gets built for AI legibility from day one (structured data included), and the weekly upkeep happens whether you think about it or not.
